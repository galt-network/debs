**1. Political Individualism (Individuals as subjects of rights / Sovereignty of the individual)**

The liberal political order takes the individual as its sole moral starting point and center. Each person is understood as a sovereign agent who deliberately forms and pursues their own life projects; the political order is therefore judged by how it affects each individual, not collectives, nature, divinity, or any other entity. This individualism recognizes rights as enforceable limits on what others may do to a person and rejects treating individuals as mere objects or instruments of groups, majorities, or abstract ideals. It is compatible with social cooperation and the importance of groups for personal development, but insists that groups derive their value from serving individuals and never the reverse.

**2. Juridical Equality (All individuals possess the same rights)**

Because every person is equally sovereign over themselves, all individuals hold identical fundamental rights regardless of religion, race, sex, nationality, sexual orientation, or any other trait. This principle of equality before the law (isonomy) prohibits legal privileges, castes, or discrimination that would grant some people rights denied to others. It also rules out “positive discrimination” or compensatory inequalities that create new legal privileges today to offset past ones. The liberal tradition has historically fought to extend this equal legal status to previously excluded groups, affirming that no one possesses a natural right to rule over others.

**3. Personal Freedom (The most basic right is liberty as a negative right)**

The foundational right is negative liberty: the right of each person to be free from violent interference by others in the pursuit of their chosen life plans. Liberty is understood as a right of non-aggression (the “principle of non-aggression”), protecting a moral space in which individuals may act—even in ways others consider mistaken or immoral—so long as they do not forcibly obstruct others. Positive rights (claims to compel others to provide goods or services) are rejected because they necessarily conflict with negative liberty. Freedom is paired with personal responsibility: individuals bear the consequences of their choices and may not shift the costs of their decisions onto others through coercion.

**4. Private Property**

To render liberty effective in a world of scarce resources, individuals possess the full right to use, enjoy, dispose of, exclude others from, and derive income from goods they have peacefully acquired—either by first occupation of unowned resources or by voluntary transfer (gift, sale, or inheritance). Property rights transform the abstract right of non-interference into concrete control over the material means necessary for life projects. The liberal conception is broad: it includes both individual and voluntary communal (private-group) ownership when the latter arises peacefully and is internally well-governed, while generally rejecting “intellectual property” claims over non-rival ideas.

**5. Contractual Autonomy (The right to bind oneself to others)**

Freedom and property enable individuals to create enforceable, voluntary obligations toward one another through contracts. Contracts allow people to structure cooperation predictably and reliably over time, making complex joint projects possible that would be too fragile if based solely on informal, revocable goodwill. Because contracts rest on mutual consent, they expand rather than restrict liberty: each party gains rights to performance by the other while remaining free to choose whether and on what terms to bind themselves. This principle underpins all voluntary, rule-governed cooperation in civil society and the economy.

**6. Restitution (Whoever violates others’ rights must repair the damage caused)**

When rights to liberty, property, or contract are violated, the victim acquires a right to reparation from the aggressor. Justice requires that the offender compensate the victim for the harm inflicted; this both restores the victim and enforces the underlying rights. The liberal view prioritizes restitution to the victim over expansive state punishment or retribution detached from compensation. Without effective reparation mechanisms, rights would be emptied of practical meaning, reducing the social order to a “law of the jungle.”

**7. Voluntary Association (The right to associate with others)**

Individuals may form stable, long-term associations (families, religious communities, clubs, civic groups, etc.) to pursue shared purposes under internally agreed rules (statutes). This freedom of association necessarily includes the freedom not to associate and the freedom to disassociate. Associations possess the autonomy to set their own internal governance provided they respect the fundamental rights of members and non-members. Voluntary association enables rich, organic cooperation for common goals while preserving each person’s sovereignty; it is the institutional expression of civil society.

**8. Free Market (The right to associate economically with others)**

Economic cooperation follows the same liberal principles: free choice of profession, private ownership of the means of production, free trade, free enterprise, and free competition. The market is the spontaneous, decentralized order that emerges when individuals interact voluntarily through property and contract to produce, exchange, and allocate goods and services. Prices and profit-and-loss signals coordinate dispersed knowledge and incentives far more effectively than central direction. Restrictions on competition (monopolies, cartels, licensing barriers, etc.) violate the free-market principle by coercively privileging some at the expense of others’ liberty.

**9. Limited Government**

Communities form political associations primarily to protect individual rights when purely private enforcement proves insufficient. Government’s legitimate functions are therefore strictly limited to specifying the concrete content of rights, using defensive force to prevent or remedy their violation (maintaining public order), and raising the minimal resources necessary for these tasks. Any expansion beyond these protective functions—into redistribution, regulation of voluntary exchanges, or provision of most goods and services—violates the rights whose protection was the government’s sole justification. The liberal state is a limited instrument, not an end in itself.

**10. Pacifism / Global Liberal Order (The liberal political order is universal)**

The same individual rights recognized domestically apply universally to all human beings; liberalism is therefore cosmopolitan. Communities that respect their own citizens’ rights deserve non-interference from outsiders. This entails rejection of offensive, imperialist wars and of any claim by one political community to impose its will on another. Internationally, the liberal order favors the globalization of voluntary civil and commercial association—free movement of people and goods, peaceful trade, and cultural exchange—while opposing coercive political integration into a world state. The consistent liberal stance in foreign affairs mirrors its domestic one: “Do not violently interfere in the lives of others; limit yourself to peaceful interaction.”
